Photosynthesis

Biological conversion of light energy into chemical energy by plants, algae, and cyanobacteria; oxygenic photosynthesis oxidizes water to O2 and fixes CO2 into carbohydrates.

Overview

Photosynthesis comprises reactions by which photoautotrophs capture light energy to generate ATP and reducing power, which are used to fix carbon dioxide into organic molecules. Oxygenic photosynthesis—common to plants, algae, and cyanobacteria—splits water, releasing O2. The carbohydrates produced (e.g., glucose, sucrose; storage as starch) fuel metabolism and growth.

Some bacteria perform anoxygenic photosynthesis, using alternative electron donors (e.g., H2S) and not producing O2. A few organisms are photoheterotrophs, using light for energy while relying on organic carbon sources.

Pigments and Light Harvesting

Chlorophyll a is the primary photopigment; accessory pigments (chlorophyll b, carotenoids, phycobilins) broaden spectral absorption and protect against photooxidative damage. Antenna complexes funnel excitation energy to reaction centers in photosystems.

Light-Dependent Reactions

In thylakoid membranes, Photosystem II (PSII) and Photosystem I (PSI) operate in series. PSII oxidizes water (photolysis), releasing O2 and electrons that travel via plastoquinone, cytochrome b6f, and plastocyanin to PSI. Proton translocation during electron transport establishes an electrochemical gradient used by ATP synthase to generate ATP (photophosphorylation). PSI reduces NADP+ to NADPH. Cyclic electron flow around PSI supplements ATP without producing NADPH.

Carbon Fixation (Calvin–Benson Cycle)

In the chloroplast stroma, RuBisCO catalyzes carboxylation of ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate to form 3-phosphoglycerate. Subsequent reduction yields triose phosphates, some exported for sucrose synthesis while the remainder regenerate ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate. ATP and NADPH from the light reactions power these steps.

C3, C4, and CAM Pathways

C3 Photosynthesis

Most plants use the C3 pathway, producing 3-carbon 3-phosphoglycerate as the first stable product. Under hot, dry conditions, stomatal closure lowers CO2, increasing RuBisCO oxygenation and photorespiration.

C4 Photosynthesis

C4 species initially fix CO2 via PEP carboxylase in mesophyll cells, forming 4-carbon acids that are decarboxylated in bundle sheath cells to elevate CO2 around RuBisCO, suppressing photorespiration and improving efficiency at high light and temperature.

CAM (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism)

CAM plants open stomata at night to fix CO2 into organic acids; by day, CO2 is released internally for the Calvin cycle while stomata remain closed, conserving water in arid environments.

Photorespiration

When RuBisCO oxygenates ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate, glycolate is formed and recycled via chloroplasts, peroxisomes, and mitochondria, consuming energy and releasing previously fixed CO2. C4 and CAM pathways mitigate photorespiration under stress.

Environmental and Physiological Factors

Photosynthetic rates depend on irradiance (intensity and spectrum), temperature, CO2 availability and diffusion, water status, nutrient supply, and leaf structure and age. Canopy architecture and shading patterns influence whole-plant and ecosystem productivity.

Measurement and Efficiency

Efficiency in field conditions is commonly ~3–6% of incident solar energy, lower under stress and approaching ~8% under optimal conditions. Chlorophyll fluorescence and gas exchange measurements provide non-invasive assessment of electron transport and CO2 assimilation, respectively.

Evolution and Endosymbiosis

Photosynthesis originated early in Earth’s history. Oxygenic photosynthesis evolved within cyanobacteria and drove atmospheric oxygenation. Eukaryotic chloroplasts arose via primary endosymbiosis of a cyanobacterium; subsequent secondary and tertiary endosymbioses spread plastids across diverse algal lineages.

Experimental History

Jan van Helmont suggested that plant mass largely arises from water. Joseph Priestley and Jan Ingenhousz linked plants, gases, and light, showing that plants restore air in light. Cornelis van Niel framed photosynthesis as a light-driven redox process. Robert Emerson’s “red drop” and subsequent work established two photosystems with different spectral properties.

Ecological and Global Significance

Oxygenic photosynthesis is the dominant source of atmospheric O2 and the principal input of free energy to the biosphere, underpinning food webs and regulating biogeochemical cycles of carbon, oxygen, and other elements.
